# Understanding the Hiring Process: Who Actually Reads Your Resume?

> This is the first lesson in the "Build Your Resume with AI" series. Before we dive into tools and techniques, let's start with the fundamentals: understanding how American companies actually hire.

## Overview

Welcome to the first lesson in this series.

Our learning path works like this: the first few lessons focus on theory and knowledge—helping you understand what makes a great resume, and why. After that, we'll move into the hands-on part: using AI tools to manage, iterate, and continuously improve your resume.

Why start with the hiring process? Because a resume isn't just a document—it's a letter written for a specific reader. If you don't know who's reading, you can't write something that resonates.

---

## Learning Objectives

Think about it: you spend hours polishing your resume, but do you actually know who's reading it?

Is it a Recruiter scanning hundreds of resumes every day? An automated system filtering by keywords? Or a Hiring Manager looking for someone to solve a specific problem?

Different answers mean completely different resume strategies.

By the end of this lesson, you will:

1. Understand three distinct hiring scenarios and how resumes flow through each one
2. Know the key players in the hiring process: who screens, who decides
3. Recognize a critical reality: when new grads apply to smaller companies, they're competing directly with experienced candidates

## Key Concepts

### The Core Question: Who's Reading Your Resume?

Before you write a single bullet point, ask yourself:

- Whose hands will this resume pass through?
- Who's screening? Who's deciding?
- What are they looking for?

Different hiring scenarios mean different readers. Understand this, and your entire approach to resume writing will change.

---

### Scenario One: Big Company Campus Recruiting

**Typical Companies:** FAANG (Meta, Amazon, Apple, Netflix, Google), Big 4 accounting firms, major banks, well-known tech companies

**Who's Hiring:** University Recruiting teams—HR departments dedicated to campus recruiting

**How It Works:**

1. Company determines next year's headcount (hiring quota)
2. University Recruiting team selects target schools (usually tiered)
3. They visit campuses for Career Fairs and Info Sessions
4. Resumes come in through school platforms (like Handshake) or company websites
5. Large volume of resumes enters the system for standardized screening (ATS + manual review)
6. Candidates who pass go through Online Assessment → Phone Screen → On-site Interview
7. Offers go out in batches, with unified start dates

**What Makes This Model Unique:**

- High-volume, assembly-line recruiting with standardized processes
- Hard metrics matter a lot: school prestige, GPA, major, keyword matching
- No specific Hiring Manager is watching you from the start—you get screened first, then assigned to a team after you're hired

**What This Means for Your Resume:** Your first hurdle is passing standardized screening. Your resume needs to "look qualified" so ATS systems and Recruiters can quickly check the boxes.

---

### Scenario Two: Small to Mid-Size Companies (New Grads Applying Directly)

**Typical Companies:** Startups, mid-size tech companies, companies without formal campus recruiting programs

**Who's Hiring:** A Hiring Manager (the actual boss) has a need and either reviews resumes themselves or has a Recruiter help screen

**How It Works:**

1. Hiring Manager needs someone (team is short-staffed, new project kicking off)
2. They write a Job Description, post it on LinkedIn, company website, Indeed
3. Resumes trickle in—could be dozens, could be hundreds
4. Recruiter or Hiring Manager personally screens them
5. Phone interview → Technical interview → On-site → Offer

**What Makes This Model Unique:**

- **New grads compete in the same pool as experienced candidates**
- There's no "campus recruiting track"—your resume sits next to someone with 2-3 years of experience
- Process is more flexible, Hiring Manager has more influence

**What This Means for Your Resume:** You need to stand out. Without work experience, you have to use projects, potential, and learning ability to make the Hiring Manager think: "This person is worth talking to."

**Important Reality Check:** Many new grads job hunting in the US are actually applying to these kinds of positions. Don't think only big company campus recruiting can land you a job. But this also means—your resume needs to be more competitive.

---

### Scenario Three: Experienced Hiring (Job Switching)

**Typical Situation:** Someone with 1-5 years of work experience changing jobs

**Who's Hiring:** Hiring Manager has specific needs, works with Internal Recruiter or Agency Recruiter

**How It Works:**

1. Hiring Manager knows exactly what they need (specific skills, experience level, seniority)
2. Recruiter posts the job, actively sources candidates
3. Resumes flood in (popular roles can get 1000+)
4. Recruiter screens down to 20-30, hands them to Hiring Manager
5. Hiring Manager picks who goes into the interview process
6. Interviews → Offer

**What Makes This Model Unique:**

- Hiring Manager has a specific problem to solve (missing a skill, filling a position)
- Your resume needs to show: you can solve their problem
- Relevant experience and concrete results matter a lot

**What This Means for Your Resume:** Show that you have relevant experience and can hit the ground running. Not "I'm impressive," but "I can help you solve X problem."

## Key Players

### Hiring Manager

- The person who actually needs to hire someone
- The one who makes the final call on whether to bring you on
- What they care about: Can this person solve my problem? Will they fit on the team?

### Recruiter

- The person helping the Hiring Manager screen resumes
- They're the gatekeeper—your resume has to pass them first
- Two types:
  - **Internal Recruiter:** Company HR, understands company culture and needs
  - **Agency Recruiter (Headhunter):** External, gets paid when you get hired

### ATS (Applicant Tracking System)

- Automated resume management system
- Helps screen, rank, and track candidates
- Next lesson will cover how ATS actually works

---

## Core Insights

**Insight One: Different scenarios mean your resume needs to impress different people**

- Big company campus recruiting: Impress ATS and Recruiters first (standardized screening)
- Small/mid-size companies: Directly impress the Hiring Manager (they might personally read your resume)
- Experienced hiring: Show you can solve the Hiring Manager's specific problem

**Insight Two: New grads applying to small/mid-size companies = going through the experienced hire process**

- You're competing against people with work experience
- Your resume needs to highlight projects and potential to compensate for lack of experience

**Insight Three: The purpose of a resume is to get an interview**

- It's not about proving you're impressive
- It's about making the reader think: "Worth having a conversation"

## Mentor's Note

**Why this lesson matters**

I've seen too many job seekers treat resume writing as a purely mechanical task—fill in a template, list responsibilities, and hope someone notices. But a resume is communication. And effective communication starts with knowing who you're talking to.

When you understand that a startup's Hiring Manager might personally read every single resume, while a big company's recruiting machine processes thousands through automated systems—your strategy changes. The words you choose change. The order of your bullet points changes.

**Key insights:**

- There's no such thing as a "one-size-fits-all great resume." There's only resumes that work in specific situations for specific readers.
- Most new grads underestimate how fierce the competition is when applying outside the campus recruiting system against experienced candidates.
- The hiring process isn't something that "happens to you." It's a system you can understand and strategically navigate.

**Key Players:**

- Recruiter: Screens resumes, acts as gatekeeper
