# New Grad Resumes: Landing Interviews When You Have Zero Experience

> This is lesson three in the "Build Your Resume with AI" series. We've covered how hiring works and how screening actually happens. Now let's get into the weeds: how do you actually write a resume that gets callbacks?

## Overview

Welcome to lesson three.

We've done a lot of groundwork in the first two lessons. You know who's reading your resume—Recruiters, Hiring Managers, different people in different situations. You know ATS is just a database, not some AI gatekeeper, and that real humans make the calls.

Now comes the hard part: actually writing a resume that gets you in the door.

This lesson is for new grads. If you're still in school or just graduated without any full-time experience under your belt, this one's for you.

Here's the catch-22 every new grad faces: resumes are supposed to show what you can do, but you haven't done anything yet. So how do you play this?

---

## Learning Objectives

Let's circle back to lesson one. As a new grad, you're looking at two main paths.

**Path one: Big tech campus recruiting.** Google, Meta, Amazon—these companies have entire teams dedicated to hiring from universities. They hit up Career Fairs, run Info Sessions, collect resumes by the thousands, and process everything like a factory line. Your resume gets thrown into a pile with everyone else's, and hard numbers matter: school name, GPA, major, the right buzzwords.

**Path two: Startups and mid-size companies.** These places don't have fancy recruiting programs. A manager needs someone, throws up a job posting, and watches the applications roll in. Here's the kicker: your resume lands in the same pile as people who've been working for 2-3 years. Most new grads don't realize this—you're not competing against other fresh faces. You're up against people with real job experience.

Different games, different rules. But the core problem is the same: how do you convince someone you're worth talking to when you've never had a real job?

By the end of this lesson, you'll know how to structure your resume, whether you need a Summary, how to make your projects actually sound impressive, and what rookie mistakes to avoid.

---

## Reality Check: You Probably Need Multiple Resumes

Before we dive into structure, here's something most new grads miss.

One resume won't cut it.

Why? Because the same job title can mean completely different things at different companies.

Take "AI Developer." At one company, that means writing production code and system integration—basically a software engineer who touches AI. At another, it means training and tuning models. At a third, it's all about data pipelines. Same title, totally different jobs.

If you blast the same resume to all three, you're not a great fit for any of them.

And if you're versatile enough to target multiple job families—Software Engineer, Data Engineer, ML Engineer—you need a separate resume for each one.

What changes? Your Summary. Which skills you lead with. Which projects you highlight. Even how you phrase your bullet points.

We'll get into tailoring later. For now, just remember: the spray-and-pray approach doesn't work anymore.

## How to Structure a New Grad Resume

Let's nail down what goes where.

For new grads, here's the order that works:

1. Contact Info
2. Summary — only if you're targeting a specific role
3. Education
4. Skills
5. Projects
6. Work Experience (internships, if you've got them)

Notice where Summary sits. If you're going after a specific type of role and need to frame yourself upfront, put it right at the top—even above Education. It's your elevator pitch: "Here's who I am and what I bring."

Let's break each section down.

---

## Contact Info

Seems simple, right? You'd be surprised how many people mess this up.

Include your name, email, phone, and city. LinkedIn and GitHub are fair game—but only if they're actually worth showing.

Here's where a lot of people shoot themselves in the foot: linking to a GitHub that's basically a graveyard. A handful of empty repos, commits from two years ago, a Heroku app that's been dead for months. That's not helping you. If your GitHub isn't polished, leave it off.

One more thing: keep everything visually clean. Misaligned URLs, weird spacing, a LinkedIn link that could've fit on one line but wraps to the next—these details matter. If you claim to be "detail-oriented," your resume better prove it.

---

## Summary: Do You Need One?

This comes up a lot: should new grads include a Summary?

Here's my take: **write one if you're targeting a specific role and need to position yourself. Skip it if you're just going to copy-paste the same generic blurb everywhere.**

A weak Summary is worse than no Summary. Like this gem:

> *Passionate developer seeking challenging opportunities to grow and contribute to innovative teams.*

That says absolutely nothing. Everyone writes that. It's resume white noise.

But a good Summary? It tells the reader exactly who you are in about five seconds.

### Where Does It Go?

Right at the top. Above Education.

Why? Because it frames everything that comes after. Before the Recruiter even starts scanning, your Summary tells them: "I'm a frontend person" or "I'm a data person" or "I'm an ML person." That context shapes how they read the rest.

### Different Targets = Different Summaries = Different Resumes

This brings us back to the multiple resume thing. Different roles need different positioning.

Say you're a CS grad who's dabbled in web dev, data analysis, and ML. You want to apply for SDE, Data Engineer, and ML Engineer roles. Here's how your Summary might look for each:

**For Software Engineer:**

> *CS new grad with hands-on experience building full-stack web apps. Solid with Python, JavaScript, React, and Node.js. Shipped 3 production projects used by 500+ people.*

You're leading with: full-stack chops, web stack, stuff that's actually live.

**For Data Engineer:**

> *CS new grad with a strong foundation in data systems and ETL pipelines. Comfortable with Python, SQL, Spark, and AWS. Built automated pipelines handling 100K+ records daily.*

Now you're all about: data infrastructure, pipeline work, scale.

**For ML Engineer:**

> *CS new grad focused on machine learning and deep learning. Proficient in Python, PyTorch, and TensorFlow. Trained CNN models hitting 94% accuracy on image classification.*

This one's about: ML/DL skills, model training, concrete metrics.

Same person. Three completely different framings.

### More Examples Across Roles

**AI Developer (building apps):**

> *CS new grad building AI-powered applications. Experienced with Python, LangChain, and OpenAI APIs. Built a RAG chatbot handling 1000+ queries in production.*

**AI Engineer (infrastructure):**

> *CS new grad specializing in ML deployment at scale. Proficient in Python, PyTorch, Docker, and Kubernetes. Built end-to-end pipelines from training to serving.*

**Data Analyst:**

> *Stats and CS new grad with strong analytical chops. Comfortable with Python, SQL, Tableau, and Excel. Delivered analysis that drove real business decisions.*

**Data Scientist:**

> *CS new grad focused on statistical modeling and ML. Experienced with Python, R, scikit-learn, and Pandas. Built predictive models with 85%+ accuracy on real datasets.*

**Cloud Engineer:**

> *CS new grad with hands-on cloud infrastructure experience. Proficient in AWS (EC2, S3, Lambda), Terraform, and Docker. Deployed apps running at 99.9% uptime.*

**Solutions Architect (rare for new grads):**

> *CS new grad with broad technical range across cloud, data, and app development. AWS Certified. Designed microservices architectures for 3 capstone projects.*

**Quant Developer:**

> *Math and CS new grad with a strong quant foundation. Proficient in Python, C++, and statistical modeling. Built a backtesting framework processing 1M+ data points.*

Every Summary follows the same formula:

1. Who you are
2. What you're good at
3. What you've actually done

But the emphasis shifts completely depending on the role.

## Education

For new grads, this is one of your strongest cards. If you don't have a Summary, Education goes first. If you do, Education comes right after.

What to include: school, degree, major, graduation date. If your GPA is solid (3.5+), include it. If not, just leave it off—nobody's going to ask why.

Relevant Coursework can help, especially if it ties to your target role. But tailor it:

**Targeting ML Engineer?**

> *Relevant Coursework: Machine Learning, Deep Learning, Statistical Learning, Linear Algebra, Probability & Stats*

**Targeting Software Engineer?**

> *Relevant Coursework: Data Structures & Algorithms, Operating Systems, Databases, Software Engineering, Networks*

**Targeting Data Engineer?**

> *Relevant Coursework: Database Systems, Distributed Systems, Data Mining, Cloud Computing, Big Data Analytics*

Also worth mentioning: any standout achievements. Won a hackathon? Led a coding club? TA'd a tough class? Throw that in. It shows you weren't just warming a seat.

---

## Skills

Keep this tight and targeted.

The biggest mistake? Listing every technology you've ever touched. Python, Java, C++, JavaScript, TypeScript, React, Vue, Angular, Node, Django, Flask, Spring, TensorFlow, PyTorch, AWS, GCP, Azure...

When you list 15 languages and 20 frameworks, you're not saying "I know a lot." You're saying "I'm probably mediocre at all of it."

Hiring managers want depth, not breadth.

Tailor your Skills to the role. Same person, different targets:

**For SDE:**

> *Languages: Python, JavaScript, TypeScript, Java*
> *Frameworks: React, Node.js, Express, Django*
> *Tools: Git, Docker, PostgreSQL, Redis*

**For Data Engineer:**

> *Languages: Python, SQL, Scala*
> *Data Tools: Spark, Airflow, dbt, Kafka*
> *Cloud: AWS (S3, Redshift, Glue), Snowflake*

**For ML Engineer:**

> *Languages: Python, C++*
> *ML Frameworks: PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn*
> *Tools: Docker, MLflow, Weights & Biases, Jupyter*

**For Cloud Engineer:**

> *Cloud Platforms: AWS (EC2, S3, Lambda, RDS), GCP*
> *Infrastructure: Terraform, Docker, Kubernetes*
> *Languages: Python, Bash, YAML*

Here's a gut check: only list what you can actually talk about in an interview. If someone asks "What did you build with Kubernetes?" and you blank out, you shouldn't have listed it.

## Projects: This Is Where You Win or Lose

For new grads, this section carries the most weight.

No work experience? Your projects ARE your experience. This is where you prove you don't just sit in class—you actually build things.

### Personal Projects vs. Homework

Let's be clear: **these are not the same thing.**

If everyone in your class built the same Blackjack game, that's not a personal project. That's an assignment. Listing it doesn't impress anyone—it tells them you don't do anything beyond the minimum.

What do employers actually want to see? People who build stuff on their own time because they're genuinely curious. People who love this enough to keep going when no one's making them.

What's worth listing? Projects that solve real problems. Projects that show technical depth. Projects that explore something interesting.

### How to Describe Projects: Action + Technical Detail + Impact

Most new grads write something like:

> *Built a To-Do List app using React and Node.js.*

Cool, but so what? You listed technologies. You didn't tell me what you actually did or why it matters.

Good project descriptions follow a formula: **Action + Technical Detail + Impact.**

**Action: What did you do?**

Start with a strong verb. But here's the thing: **stay in your lane with word choice.**

Words like "Spearheaded," "Architected," "Pioneered"? Those imply senior leadership. If you're a student who built a class project solo, using those words sounds ridiculous.

Stick with these:

- Built
- Developed
- Implemented
- Designed
- Created
- Optimized
- Automated
- Integrated

Strong enough to sound capable. Not so strong you sound delusional.

**Technical Detail: How did you build it?**

Don't just list tech—show that you understand how the pieces fit together.

Weak:

> *Used React, Node.js, MongoDB, Express.*

Better:

> *Built a responsive frontend with React and TypeScript, RESTful API with Node.js/Express, and used MongoDB for persistence.*

The second one shows you actually get what each technology does.

**Impact: What was the result?**

This is where most people either skip it entirely or get themselves in trouble.

Here's the rule: **if you can't defend it in an interview, don't write it.**

If you claim "reduced page load time by 50%," be ready for:

- How'd you measure that?
- What was it before and after?
- What tool did you use?
- What specific change caused the improvement?

If you can't answer these, that number becomes a red flag. Now they're wondering what else on your resume is BS.

Before you write any metric, ask yourself:

- Is this number realistic for the industry?
- Can I explain how I measured it?
- Can I walk through the details if pressed?

If you're not sure, talk it through with AI first. Something like:

> "I optimized image loading and got page load from 3 seconds to 1.5 seconds. Is that believable? I used Lighthouse in Chrome DevTools—is that legit?"

If you genuinely don't have good numbers, describe what problem you solved instead. Impact without metrics still counts:

> *Chrome extension that matches Spotify songs by BPM for workout playlists—no more manual curation.*

No numbers, but it clearly solves a real problem.

## Work Experience (Internships)

If you've interned, this section is gold.

Even a summer gig is proof that someone actually paid you to do work. That's fundamentally different from class projects.

### Show Ownership

Make it clear this was YOUR work, not something you just "participated in."

Weak:

> *Participated in the team's data analysis project.*

Better:

> *Owned the data cleaning module, processing 100K+ customer records daily.*

Words like "Owned," "Led," "Responsible for" signal that you had real skin in the game.

But keep it honest. If you only touched one small piece, don't write it like you ran the show.

### Quantify (Carefully)

Internships give you access to real business metrics. Same rule applies though: only include numbers you can back up.

Good examples:

> *Automated report generation, cutting time from 2 hours to 15 minutes.*
>
> *Improved model inference speed 30% through caching and code optimization.*
>
> *Contributed to a feature shipped to 10K+ users.*

These are all defensible—you know the before and after, you can explain what you did.

### Watch the Confidentiality

Assume internal data is confidential. Before putting specific numbers on your resume, either get permission or use percentages instead of raw figures.

Don't say: "DAU grew from 1M to 1.5M."

Say: "DAU increased 50%."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Here's what I see new grads mess up most often.

### Mistake 1: The Technology Laundry List

Listing 15 languages and 20 frameworks signals you're probably mediocre at all of them. Pick the 3-5 that matter most for your target role.

### Mistake 2: Passing Off Homework as Personal Projects

If everyone in your class built it, it's not a personal project. You need at least one thing you built because you wanted to, not because you had to.

### Mistake 3: All Tech, No Substance

> *Built an app using React, Node.js, MongoDB.*

That tells me nothing. What did it do? What problem did it solve? What happened when people used it?

### Mistake 4: Unbelievable Numbers

> *Improved performance by 500%.*

If you can't explain how you measured that, what it was before, what it was after, and what you changed—don't write it.

### Mistake 5: Overblown Language

"Spearheaded," "Architected," "Pioneered"—these suggest senior leadership roles. If you're a student who did a solo project, this just sounds fake. "Built," "Developed," "Implemented" work fine.

### Mistake 6: Dead GitHub Links

If your GitHub is empty or hasn't been touched in two years, don't link it. A bad GitHub is worse than no GitHub.

### Mistake 7: Two-Page Resume

You're a new grad. You don't need two pages. If you think you do, you're not being concise enough. Recruiters spend maybe six seconds per resume. If they have to flip the page, you've already lost.

### Mistake 8: One Resume for Everything

Same resume for SDE, Data Engineer, and ML roles? You'll be mediocre for all of them. Tailored versions actually get callbacks.

## Different Strategies for Different Games

Remember those two scenarios from the beginning? Here's how to play each one.

### Big Tech Campus Recruiting

This is a numbers game. Your resume is one of thousands getting quick scans.

Your job: check all the boxes. School, GPA, keyword matches—the hard metrics matter here. Make it easy for Recruiters to give you a thumbs up. Big companies are looking for "qualified," not "unique." Cover the basics: right major, relevant skills, solid projects. If the JD mentions Java and Spring, make sure those show up in your Skills and Projects.

### Startups and Mid-Size Companies

No dedicated recruiting pipeline. Your resume lands next to people with actual work experience.

Your job: stand out. "Qualified" isn't enough when you're up against someone with three years in. You need to make the Hiring Manager think "let's give this person a shot."

Show potential. You don't have experience, but you can demonstrate that you learn fast, take initiative, and solve real problems. Your personal projects matter way more here.

Tell a story. Why should they take a chance on someone with no track record? Your projects, your depth, how you think about problems—that's what makes the case.

Both scenarios care about clarity, impact, and scannability. The difference is where you put the emphasis.

---

## Key Takeaways

**One: You need multiple resumes.** Different roles need different versions with different Summaries, Skills, and project ordering.

**Two: Summary goes at the top if you write one.** Its job is to frame who you are before they read anything else.

**Three: Projects are everything for new grads.** They're the only proof you can actually build things.

**Four: Use Action + Technical Detail + Impact for every project.** Real verbs, real details, real results. No fluff.

**Five: Only write numbers you can defend.** If you can't survive the follow-up questions, leave it out.

**Six: Keep Skills tight and targeted.** A focused list beats an everything-bagel list every time.

## Mentor's Note

I've seen plenty of new grads who are genuinely capable but have resumes that look like they threw them together in 15 minutes.

They dump a wall of technologies but never show what they built. They list every class project but have nothing they made on their own. Their metrics look impressive until someone asks a follow-up question.

This isn't a skills problem. It's a communication problem.

Your resume answers one question: Why should we talk to this person?

For new grads, the answer isn't "because I've learned a lot of stuff." It's "because I can actually do things."

How do you prove you can do things? By showing things you've done.

That's why personal projects matter so much. They're the only place you can demonstrate "I didn't just learn this—I shipped something with it."

If your current projects aren't strong enough for a solid resume, that's your signal: go build something.

Find a problem you actually care about. Solve it with the skills you've learned. Finish it, deploy it, get people using it. The building is the learning, and you walk away with something real to put on paper.

Before you write any numbers, think through how you'd explain them. Talk it out with AI, run experiments, look up industry benchmarks. Do the homework upfront so every line on your resume can survive scrutiny.

People who genuinely love this stuff build more and get better faster. Let your resume prove you're one of them.
