# Resumes with 1-5 Years of Experience: Stop Listing Duties, Start Showing Results

> Lesson four in the "Build Your Resume with AI" series. Last time we tackled new grad resumes. Now it's time for people who've actually been in the trenches—specifically the 1-5 year crowd.

## Overview

Welcome to lesson four.

If you've got a few years under your belt and you're thinking about jumping ship—new company, new direction, whatever—this one's for you.

Here's the thing: once you have experience, the whole resume game flips.

New grads are stuck figuring out "I've never had a real job, how do I prove I can do this?" That's not your problem. You've got experience. You've shipped stuff. The real question is: **how do you show you actually moved the needle?**

Too many experienced folks still write resumes like they're filling out a job description. "Responsible for development and maintenance of XX system." "Participated in design and implementation of XX project." Snooze. These tell people what you did at work, but not whether you were any good at it.

Let's fix that.

---

## Learning Objectives

Quick refresher from lesson one. When you've got experience, you're playing the experienced hire game.

Some Hiring Manager has a hole to fill—missing a skill, backfilling a role, solving a headache. Recruiters blast out the job posting and start hunting. Applications pile up—hot roles can hit 1000+ easy. Recruiters whittle it down to 20-30 decent ones and toss them to the Hiring Manager. The Hiring Manager cherry-picks who gets to interview.

So what's the Hiring Manager actually looking for?

**Someone who can solve their problem.**

Not "you seem smart." Not "you've touched a lot of technologies." But "you can help me fix X."

Your resume needs to scream: I've done this before, I can hit the ground running, and I've got the receipts to prove it.

By the time you finish this lesson, you'll know how to structure an experienced resume, how to write work experience that actually pops, how to tailor for different gigs, and what landmines to avoid.

---

## What Changes Once You've Got Experience?

Before we get tactical, let's talk about what's different now that you're not fresh out of school.

### Work Experience Takes Center Stage

For new grads, Projects carry the resume. No job history? Projects are all you've got.

But now? **Your work history IS the story.** You don't need side projects to fill space anymore. Your 9-to-5 is the most convincing thing on the page.

That doesn't mean Projects are dead weight. Got a sick side project? Open source cred? Something that's dead-on relevant to where you're applying? Sure, throw it in. But it's gravy now, not the main course.

### Education Gets Demoted

New grads put Education at the top because it's their strongest card.

If you've got 3-4 years in the game? **Shove Education to the bottom.** Your work history tells a way better story than your diploma. Hiring Managers care infinitely more about what you built at Google than where you went to school.

Okay, maybe if you went to MIT or Stanford you keep it visible. But even then, Work Experience still wins by a landslide.

### You've Earned the Big Words

In the new grad lesson, I said stay away from words like "Spearheaded" and "Architected." Coming from someone who solo'd a class project, those words sound ridiculous.

But if you've actually been around the block—actually led teams, actually made architecture calls—go ahead and use them.

The rule: **your words have to match your actual experience.**

If you're an IC who mostly executes what other people design, don't claim you "Led a team of 5 engineers." But if you genuinely drove projects, brought up junior devs, or made technical calls that stuck, make that crystal clear.

### Numbers Are Non-Negotiable

New grad project metrics are often fuzzy—personal projects and school assignments don't exactly come with business dashboards.

You've got work experience. You should have real numbers.

"Cut API response time from 500ms to 200ms."
"Refactor dropped bug reports by 40%."
"My feature bumped DAU 25% after launch."

These numbers are your heavy artillery. We'll dig into where to find them and how to present them.

## How to Structure an Experienced Resume

Let's lock down the format.

Here's the lineup:

1. Contact Info
2. Summary — tells them who you are and what you bring
3. Skills — upfront so they can quickly sniff-test the fit
4. Work Experience — the meat
5. Projects — optional, only if they're a slam dunk
6. Education — at the very bottom

Biggest shift from new grad resumes: Work Experience is the headliner now, and Education rides the bench.

Let's break it down.

---

## Summary: Who the Hell Are You?

For experienced folks, Summary is basically required.

Why? Because you've got a professional identity now. You're not some new grad who's "dabbled in a bit of everything." You're a professional with a lane. Summary's job is to tell people right off the bat: this is a Backend Engineer, a Data Engineer, an ML Engineer, with X years in, strong at Y and Z.

A killer Summary hits three notes:

1. Your professional identity and tenure
2. Your core skills or specialty
3. A standout win (optional but adds juice)

### Summary Examples by Role

**Senior Software Engineer (Backend):**

> *Backend Engineer with 4 years building scalable distributed systems. Strong in Java, Go, and AWS. Led the monolith-to-microservices migration that cut deployment time 60% and pushed uptime to 99.9%.*

What this says: 4 years deep, backend lane, distributed systems, hard numbers that back it up.

**Senior Software Engineer (Full-Stack):**

> *Full-Stack Engineer with 3 years shipping user-facing products. Solid with React, Node.js, and PostgreSQL. Built and launched 5 features now serving 500K+ monthly active users.*

**Data Engineer:**

> *Data Engineer with 4 years building and tuning data pipelines at scale. Deep in Python, Spark, and Airflow. Architected a real-time pipeline crunching 10M+ events daily at 99.9% reliability.*

**Machine Learning Engineer:**

> *ML Engineer with 3 years deploying models in production. Strong in Python, PyTorch, and MLOps tooling. Built rec systems serving 1M+ daily predictions at <100ms latency.*

**Data Scientist:**

> *Data Scientist with 4 years in predictive modeling and experimentation. Expert in Python, SQL, and statistical analysis. Ran A/B testing programs that drove $2M+ in incremental annual revenue.*

**Cloud Engineer / DevOps:**

> *Cloud Engineer with 3 years running production infrastructure on AWS. Deep in Terraform, Kubernetes, and CI/CD. Slashed infrastructure costs 30% while bumping deployment frequency 5x.*

**AI Engineer:**

> *AI Engineer with 3 years building LLM-powered apps. Strong in Python, LangChain, and vector databases. Built a RAG system that cut support ticket volume 35%.*

**Solutions Architect:**

> *Solutions Architect with 5 years designing enterprise cloud setups. AWS Certified Solutions Architect Professional. Led cloud migrations totaling $10M+ in infrastructure, delivering 40% cost savings.*

**Quant Developer:**

> *Quant Developer with 4 years in trading systems and risk analytics. Strong in Python, C++, and SQL. Built low-latency trading infra processing 50K+ orders per second.*

See how each one follows the same skeleton but highlights totally different stuff? That's tailoring doing its job.

### Summary Lives at the Top

Same deal as new grads: Summary goes first. Above Skills. Above Work Experience.

Because Summary sets the frame. Before the Recruiter even starts skimming, your Summary tells them: what kind of role you fit, what level you're at, what you bring to the table. That context colors everything they read next.

## Skills: Keep It Tight

Same playbook as new grads: **precise, targeted, only stuff you can actually riff on in an interview.**

But here's the extra wrinkle for experienced folks: your Skills need backup in your Work Experience.

If Kubernetes shows up in your Skills, readers expect to see you actually wrangling Kubernetes somewhere in your Work Experience. If you just followed a tutorial once and never touched it on the job, leave it out.

### Skills by Role

**Backend Engineer:**

> *Languages: Java, Go, Python*
> *Frameworks: Spring Boot, gRPC, GraphQL*
> *Infrastructure: AWS (EC2, RDS, SQS), Docker, Kubernetes*
> *Databases: PostgreSQL, Redis, DynamoDB*

**Full-Stack Engineer:**

> *Frontend: React, TypeScript, Next.js*
> *Backend: Node.js, Python, Django*
> *Databases: PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Redis*
> *Cloud: AWS, Vercel, Docker*

**Data Engineer:**

> *Languages: Python, SQL, Scala*
> *Data Tools: Spark, Airflow, dbt, Kafka*
> *Cloud: AWS (S3, Redshift, Glue, EMR), Snowflake*

> *Languages: Python, C++*

> *MLOps: MLflow, Kubeflow, Weights & Biases*

Skills slot in after Summary, before Work Experience. Their job is to let readers quickly gut-check: does this person's stack match what we need? If yes, they'll keep reading.

## Work Experience: Where You Win or Lose

This is the whole ballgame for experienced people.

Your job history is your strongest proof. The trick is: how do you write it so people actually see your value?

### The Formula: Action + Technical Detail + Measurable Impact

Same formula as new grad project bullets, but you can go deeper.

**Action + Technical Detail + Measurable Impact**

**Action: Claim Your Territory**

You've got experience now. Time to show ownership.

Weak:

> *Worked on the payment system.*

Strong:

> *Led the payment system redesign, coordinating across 3 cross-functional teams.*

Words like "Led," "Owned," "Drove," "Architected" signal you weren't just along for the ride—you were calling shots and making things happen.

But keep it honest. If you were just one cog on a team and didn't actually lead anything, stick with "Contributed to" or "Collaborated on." Puffing up your role will blow up in your face when you're sitting across from an interviewer.

**Technical Detail: Flex the Depth**

Technical details should show you actually know what you're doing, not just rattle off buzzwords.

Weak:

> *Used Java, Spring Boot, AWS, Kubernetes, Docker, Redis.*

Strong:

> *Built a microservices architecture on Spring Boot and gRPC, deployed on Kubernetes with auto-scaling tied to traffic patterns.*

The second version shows you get how the pieces fit together—not just that you've heard of them.

**Measurable Impact: Let the Numbers Do the Talking**

This is your unfair advantage as an experienced hire: you've got real business data.

Weak:

> *Improved system performance significantly.*

Strong:

> *Cut API latency from 500ms to 150ms (70% drop), driving a 15% bump in user engagement.*

Hard numbers crush vague "significant improvements" every single time.

### Where to Dig Up Your Impact Numbers

Lots of people say "I don't know what impact my work had." Trust me, the impact exists—you just haven't been keeping score.

Here's how to track it down.

**Inside the company:**

Your day-to-day has way more data than you think. Grab coffee with your Manager and ask about the business impact of stuff you've worked on. Chat up your PM—they usually have post-launch numbers. Check out the team's dashboards—DAU, conversion rates, error rates. Flip through All-Hands or Quarterly Review decks—they often call out project wins. Dig up A/B test results you were part of.

**Outside the company:**

Some stuff is public. Company blog posts and press releases. App Store downloads and ratings. For public companies: earnings calls and 10-K filings. Your coworkers' LinkedIn profiles—see how they frame similar projects.

**Watch the confidentiality:**

Big caveat: assume internal data is confidential.

Before slapping specific numbers on your resume, run it by your Manager. If that's awkward, use percentages or multiples instead of raw figures.

For example:

- Don't: "Bumped DAU from 1M to 1.5M"
- Do: "Bumped DAU 50%"

- Don't: "Cut infra costs from $100K/month to $70K/month"
- Do: "Cut infra costs 30%"

**What if you legit don't have numbers?**

Sometimes impact is genuinely hard to pin down—maybe you were on infrastructure, or the project's still in flight.

In those cases, describe the scale and gnarliness of what you built.

> *Architected a new auth system supporting 10M+ accounts and 50+ microservices.*

> *Built the core data pipeline powering the company's main analytics platform, processing 5TB daily.*

No "improved X by Y%" outcome, but it shows the scope and your fingerprints on it.

## Tailoring: Same Story, Different Angle

Just like new grads, experienced folks need different resume versions for different roles.

But you can slice it finer because you've got more material to play with.

### Same Job, Different Spin

Say you did backend, data work, and some ML at the same gig. For different targets, spotlight different bullets.

**Going for Backend Engineer:**

> *Led monolith-to-microservices migration, cutting deploy time 60%.*
>
> *Designed high-throughput API endpoints handling 10K+ RPS.*
>
> *Tuned database queries, slashing average response time 70%.*

**Going for Data Engineer:**

> *Built real-time pipelines on Kafka and Spark, processing 5M+ daily events.*
>
> *Designed data models and ETL workflows for an analytics platform used by 50+ internal users.*
>
> *Implemented data quality monitoring—incidents down 60%.*

**Going for ML Engineer:**

> *Built and deployed ML models for user behavior prediction—1M+ daily inferences.*
>
> *Developed feature engineering pipelines processing large-scale user data.*
>
> *Shipped an A/B testing framework for model eval and iteration.*

Same job, three totally different framings.

### Summary and Skills Shift Too

Naturally, Summary and Skills need to match. Backend target? Lead with backend positioning. Data target? Swap to data framing.

That's why you need multiple resume versions. One per direction, each dialed in for that target.

## Mistakes That'll Tank Your Resume

Here's where experienced folks trip up most often.

### Mistake 1: Listing Duties Instead of Results

> *Responsible for maintaining the payment system.*
>
> *Participated in code reviews and team meetings.*
>
> *Worked on various bug fixes and feature requests.*

This tells people what you did at work, not whether you were any good. Everyone in that seat does this stuff. The question is: what happened because of you?

### Mistake 2: Zero Quantification

> *Improved system performance significantly.*
>
> *Reduced bugs and improved code quality.*
>
> *Increased user engagement.*

"Significant," "better," "more"—these mean nothing. Use actual numbers: how much better? From what to what? How'd you measure it?

### Mistake 3: Tech Stack Dump

> *Proficient in Java, Python, Go, JavaScript, TypeScript, C++, Ruby, Scala, Kotlin, Swift, Rust, and 15 other languages.*

If your list is that long, nobody believes you're actually good at any of it. Focus on what you're genuinely strong at that matters for the role.

### Mistake 4: Education Still at the Top

3-4 years in and Education is still leading? That signals your work history isn't strong enough to go first.

Put Education at the bottom. Your work is the headliner now.

### Mistake 5: Resume Spilling Past One Page

Unless you've got 10+ years, one page does the job.

Lots of people feel like they've done so much it can't possibly fit. But Recruiters spend about 6 seconds per resume. The goal isn't to catalog everything—it's to showcase the most relevant, highest-impact stuff.

Tighten it up. Every bullet should answer "why should we interview this person?" If it doesn't, axe it.

### Mistake 6: Same Resume for Every Application

Blasting the same resume for Backend, Data, and ML roles? You'll be mediocre for all of them.

Put in the time for different versions. Tailoring seriously moves the needle on interview rates.

## Key Takeaways

**One: Work Experience is the whole ballgame now.** You don't need projects to prove yourself—your job history is the proof.

**Two: Education gets benched.** Your work speaks louder than your diploma.

**Three: Show results, not responsibilities.** Don't describe what your job was—describe what happened because you were there.

**Four: Numbers win arguments.** You've got real data—use it. "Dropped latency 70%" crushes "significantly improved performance" every time.

**Five: Start keeping score now.** Talk to your Manager and PM, bookmark dashboards, screenshot All-Hands wins. Do this before you need to write a resume.

**Six: Different roles need different resumes.** Same experience, different framing. Tailoring is a force multiplier.

## Mentor's Note

I've seen plenty of experienced engineers doing legit valuable work with resumes that read like a snooze-fest.

What's the problem? They don't know how to surface their wins.

They list duties—"responsible for building XX system," "participated in XX project." That tells people what they did, not whether they were any good.

The more common problem: they never tracked their wins in the first place. Project ships, they're on to the next thing. Years later when they need a resume, the specifics are gone.

Here's the big one: **start keeping score now.**

Every time a project ships, jot down the results. Every optimization, note the before and after. Every All-Hands that mentions your work, screenshot it.

Those notes become your best ammunition when resume time comes.

One more thing: don't sleep on tailoring. Yeah, building multiple resume versions is a pain. But think about it: you might apply to dozens of jobs before landing a handful of interviews. If tailoring bumps your hit rate from 5% to 15%, that's 3x. A few hours building different versions is absolutely worth it.

Your resume answers one question: Why should we talk to this person?

For experienced folks, the answer is: because I've done this before and I've got the receipts. Let your resume prove it.
