# Teaching Guide: Writing Your Resume Summary with AI

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this lesson, learners should be able to:

1. **Cognitive** - Understand that a Summary is a positioning statement, not a generic introduction, and that the same person can position themselves differently for different roles
2. **Skill** - Use `/summary-writer` and `/summary-reviewer` commands to produce and evaluate Summaries through an iterative workflow
3. **Mindset** - Embrace iteration as normal (not a sign of failure) and view AI as a tool that accelerates thinking, not a replacement for judgment

## Concept Sequence

Teach concepts in this order:

### Phase 1: Foundation (Review)

1. **Summary purpose** - Quick recap from previous lessons: Summary tells the reader who you are, what you can do, and why they should keep reading
2. **Tailoring concept** - Same resume body, different Summaries for different directions. This is the simplest form of Tailoring

### Phase 2: Tool Introduction

3. **Profile as raw material** - The AI needs detailed background info to work with. Garbage in, garbage out. More detail = better positioning
4. **summary-writer workflow** - Interactive brainstorming → direction identification → guided questions → draft output
5. **summary-reviewer workflow** - Harsh interviewer persona → five evaluation dimensions → scores and rewrite suggestions

### Phase 3: Integrated Workflow

6. **Two-window workflow** - One writes, one reviews. The tension between coach and critic produces better output than either alone
7. **Iteration as process** - 2-3 rounds is normal. First draft is never final. Feedback → revision → repeat

### Phase 4: Application (Assignment)

8. **Multiple positioning** - Same person, different angles: startup vs big tech, product-focused vs data-focused, AI-native vs traditional
9. **Building a toolkit** - 3+ Summary versions become reusable assets for future applications

## Common Struggles

**Struggle:** Student gives minimal profile info, expects AI to produce great output
- **Signs:** Profile has only job titles and generic descriptions; Summary output is generic and buzzwordy
- **Intervention:** Show them a before/after example. Have them add specific project details, tech choices, outcomes. Re-run and compare

**Struggle:** Student gets defensive about reviewer feedback
- **Signs:** Dismisses criticism as "the AI doesn't understand," doesn't iterate
- **Intervention:** Remind them this is a simulated tough interviewer. Real hiring managers think these things but don't say them. Better to hear it now than get silently passed over

**Struggle:** Student can't differentiate positioning angles
- **Signs:** Three "different" Summaries all say basically the same thing with minor wording changes
- **Intervention:** Ask: "If someone read these three, would they think you're applying for three different types of jobs?" Have them pick genuinely different target roles (startup vs enterprise, product vs infra) and rewrite

**Struggle:** Student over-relies on AI, doesn't edit
- **Signs:** Submits AI output verbatim, including awkward phrasing or inflated claims
- **Intervention:** Ask them to read it aloud. "Does this sound like you?" Have them cut one thing they don't actually want to claim

**Struggle:** Summary is too long
- **Signs:** Three or more lines, packed with adjectives
- **Intervention:** Set a hard constraint: "Cut this to two lines max." Force them to prioritize what matters most

## Teaching Tips

- **Start with the profile** - Before diving into tools, spend time on profile preparation. This is where most students underinvest
- **Demo the workflow live** - Show the two-window setup in action. Let students see the iteration loop before they try it
- **Normalize imperfect first drafts** - Tell them: "Your first draft will be bad. That's fine. The point is to iterate." Removes pressure
- **Use their real JDs** - Have students find an actual job posting they're interested in. Makes the exercise immediately relevant
- **Check Summary length in context** - After they finish, have them paste the Summary into an actual resume document. Does it fit? Does it look right?
- **Celebrate positioning clarity** - When a student articulates a clear angle ("I want to position as someone who ships fast at startups"), acknowledge it. Clarity is hard-won

## Assessment Ideas

- **Profile quality check:** Can you identify at least 5 specific projects/achievements from their profile? If not, it's too sparse
- **Tool mastery:** Ask them to walk you through their iteration history. Can they explain what changed between rounds and why?
- **Positioning test:** Read their 3 Summaries without labels. Can you guess which direction each one targets?
- **Credibility probe:** Pick one claim from their Summary. Ask: "If I asked you about this in an interview, what would you say?" If they hesitate, the claim might be inflated
- **Self-awareness:** Ask: "What did the reviewer criticize that you agreed with? What did you disagree with and keep anyway?" Shows they're engaging critically, not blindly following
